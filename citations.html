Secondary sources

Abbott, Elizabeth. Sugar: a Bittersweet History. Duckworth Overlook, 2008. Similar to Big Sugar, this book used in both the paper and visual was written by a research associate at Trinity College, Abbott provides an overarching view on how sugar began in a humble place reserved for the privileged few and expanded into the multibillion dollar industry today that holds such a stronghold upon many facets of our lives.

BBC Worldwide. “Where Did Sugar Come From? - Addicted To Pleasure - BBC.” YouTube, YouTube, 15 Aug. 2015, www.youtube.com/watch?v=p4g6sReVCYQ. This documentary utilized in both the paper and the visual helps illustrate the more humble beginnings of sugar, as well as its links to the classical era without being Eurocentric. BBC provides valuable insight on how sugar was a status symbol in pre-industrialization Europe.

Brenner, Marie. “In the Kingdom of Big Sugar.” The Hive, Vanity Fair, 31 Jan. 2015, www.vanityfair.com/news/2001/02/floridas-fanjuls-200102. This opinion article helps illustrate how opponents who challenged the sugar industry were becoming more outspoken critics of its atrocities. It is a primary source contributing evidence towards the point of growing opposition to the sugar industry and its crimes. This was used in both the visual and the paper.

Firger, Jessica. “Soda and Other Sugary Beverages Are Getting Cheaper Worldwide-Posing Severe Health Risks.” Newsweek, 25 May 2017, www.newsweek.com/sugar-sweetened-beverages-soda-cheaper-obesity-cancer-diabetes-594827. This news article provides other evidence illustrating how omnipotent the sugar industry can be, with from their lobbying, how some soda drinks can be priced lower than water. Although there are ads and it is on a site run for profit, its citations are explicit with figures from reputable studies. This was used in both the visual and paper.

“History of Capitalism.” Wikipedia, Wikimedia Foundation, 1 May 2018, en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_capitalism. This source used in the paper provided general overarching information about the History of Capitalism and a stepping stone to branch off of and find other more narrowed sources.

“History of Slavery.” Wikipedia, Wikimedia Foundation, 18 May 2018, en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_slavery#Britain. This source used in the paper provided general overarching information about the History of Slavery and a stepping stone to branch off of and find other more narrowed sources.

“History of Sugar.” Wikipedia, Wikimedia Foundation, 15 May 2018, en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_sugar. This source used in the paper provided general overarching information about the History of Sugar and a stepping stone to branch off of and find other more narrowed sources.

Ludwig, David S, et al. “Relation between Consumption of Sugar-Sweetened Drinks and Childhood Obesity: a Prospective, Observational Analysis.” The Lancet, vol. 357, no. 9255, 2001, pp. 505–508., doi:10.1016/s0140-6736(00)04041-1. This Harvard Medical school thesis article provides empirical evidence to illustrate the relationship between the consumption of sugar-sweetened drinks and the rising prevalence of childhood obesity. It is clearly scholarly and a reputable source, as it is published in an academic journal. This source was used in both the visual and paper.

Mckenna, Brian. “‘Big Sugar’, Sweet, White and Deadly” YouTube, YouTube, 30 Aug. 2010, www.youtube.com/watch?v=8hcAjyOFX1M. This documentary used in both the visual and the paper holds a critical lens towards how “Big Sugar” grappled with power and withheld information from the people through manipulation and deceit, and how it still does to this very day. The movie interviewed a variety of different politicians, experts, nutritionists, school children and scientists from both “Big Sugar” as well as those who fought against it. Sources are utilized visually, however none are clearly cited from the free youtube page.

Mintz, Sidney Wilfred. Sweetness and Power; The Place of Sugar in Modern History. Viking, 1985. This book used in the paper shrewdly writes about how the sugar industry has evolved from slavery through advancements made by technology during the Industrial Revolution, and contains insights on how Jamaica and Haiti were impacted by plantations in general. It provides a supplement to Abbott’s Sugar: A Bittersweet History. Mintz was a Ph.D. student at Columbia University and sources are clearly cited in the back.

Strayer, Robert W., and Eric Nelson. Ways of the World: A Global History with Sources. Bedford, Freeman Worth Publishers, 2016. Strayer provides more of a general scope of history and does not focus on sugar in particular. However the information Strayer provides on the workings of the Industrial Revolution and capitalism provides one with a foundation and a stepping stone to build off of in order to fully comprehend more complicated and nuanced sources. This source was used in both the paper and visual as scaffolding in which to build off of.

“Sweet Comparisons: How Much Sugar Is in That Drink?” CNN, Cable News Network, 30 June 2015, www.cnn.com/2014/07/02/health/gallery/sugar-sweetened-beverages/index.html. Another news article in a more graphic format provides concrete numbers as to how sugary soft drinks can be and compares them to sugary treats. Citations are explicitly stated before the slideshow. This was used in both the paper and visual.

Williams, Eric, and Colin Palmer. Capitalism and Slavery. The University of North Carolina Press, 1994. This book used in the paper also illustrates how slavery helped finance the Industrial Revolution in England along with how the slave trade allowed sugar barons to accumulate vast fortunes, to establish heavy infrastructure, and banks to spread Capitalism worldwide. It provides a critical commentary on Imperialism with a moral argument. Williams has an educational background from Oxford with his advanced research in history.
