About

Few people realize that the sweet tasting soluble carbohydrates in today’s world that passes as common table sugar and exists in some form in almost every product we ingest, have had such an arduous and violent history, along with such enduring consequences. Sugar is synonymous with oil in the pre-modern world. Just as oil greatly determines what happens in the world today, i.e. what drives countries to try to gain influence and hegemony in other areas of the world, what wars are fought, etc., so too did sugar lead global affairs, influence economic policies of nations, drive international trade, subjugate entire peoples, and wreck havoc with the environment.

other than perhaps gold
no single substance had a greater hand in shaping the
history of the Western Hemisphere than sugar, (Big Sugar 1:29)

What began as the luxury of nobles has become a “staple of every home in the modern world” (Abbott 4) and is present in just about every processed food we eat. Sugar was the heart of slavery. In the 18th century, sugar was the target of Progressives because it was so intricately tied to slavery. Today, we’re fighting sugar for the obesity epidemic and exploitation of those in poverty. The sugar lobby cast doubt over the science linking sugar and obesity, sugar and diabetes, and sugar and tooth decay. The sugar industry has a remarkable access to power, giving magnanimously to both political parties (Big Sugar 2:37). Sugar shapes politics, health and history and has had great influence on the modern world since the Age of Enlightenment.

Now sugar is not only devastating for those who harvest it, but deadly for those who consume too much. The battle against the sugar industry in the 21rst century has its roots in the 18th century when the sugar industry was at the heart and main driver of slavery. Sugar slaves toiled in agonizing conditions, brought to foriegn countries against their will to endure back breaking labor, while sugar slaves today have no choice but to buy confectionaries that are not only more delicious but are only what one can afford, leading themselves to a lifetime of immobility and pain.

The Africanization of sugar was one of the darkest hours in the sugar industry’s history. Cutting cane by hand was and still is one of the hardest forms of labor on Earth (Big Sugar 27:11) , with slaves for the most part being worked to death at a fairly early age. Imperialism was fueled by the ever growing sugar industry in Britian in the modern period.

The sugar industry in the Western world contributed greatly to the idea of the Rise of the West and through its invisible hands shapes it so, moving peoples across continents to toil until death, satisfying a sweet tooth in Britain that tossed profits of millions into the pockets of the Capitalists of sugar, funding the Western world’s industrialization, and changing the world so that there are more who gourmandize in the luxury of sugary, unhealthy processed foods than those who are without food at all.

The sugar industry was able to reach the proletariat masses through industrialization and was enabled by dormant coal beds to fuel future factories in Great Britain and led to the insufferable demand that drove plantation owners to exploit entire peoples in the West Indies, condemning peoples to a life of suffering and obesity. As a contingency of history, those in Africa and the Caribbean hold a different skin color and live in a climate suitable for sugar cultivation. One might make the mistake in saying that the Europeans enslaved people of color because they were racist, however racism and European exceptionalism arose as a justification and a means to dehumanize the cruelty and abuse onto those who were wrenched from their homeland and families to work on a farm they’ve never known.
